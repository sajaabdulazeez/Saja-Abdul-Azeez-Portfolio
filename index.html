About Me

I didn't set out to write code. I set out to understand how clothes are made — and somewhere between the cutting floor and the classroom, I realised that the most interesting problems in fashion aren't about aesthetics. They're about systems.

My work lives at the intersection of material craft and production logic. I think in fabrics and flowcharts — equally at home dissecting a seam construction and debugging a classifier. What pulls me forward is the space where textile knowledge and computational thinking meet, because that's where the real inefficiencies hide, and where the most useful work gets done.

My most significant project started as a graduation thesis and ended up as infrastructure. I built a Python Flask application backed by a Random Forest classifier to automate sole cutting planning at Walkaroo International — a decision process that 130 planners were handling manually, every day, across 13 factory units. The model reduced planning time by 87.5%, cut material waste by nearly 90%, and dropped replanning instances by 86.7%. But the part I'm most proud of isn't the numbers. It's that planners trusted it. Adoption wasn't mandated — it held because the tool was built around how people actually work on a factory floor.

Before that, I catalogued 500+ fabrics into a structured digital library at Silver Spark (Raymond Group), standardised knitting quality control at Royal Classic Mills through time-and-motion studies and SOPs, and have since been delivering tech packs for woven and knit garments as a Freelance Tech Pack Designer — turning construction knowledge into documentation manufacturers can actually use.

I'm drawn to work that demands both precision and intuition. The kind where you need to know why a fabric drapes a certain way and how to model the decision that depends on it.
